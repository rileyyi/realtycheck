Interview Prep

Product Ops Framework

Building from Nothing

The core "build from nothing" story. They want to hear that you spotted the need, made judgment calls on scope, and validated before scaling.

"We didn't have a blueprint. I figured it out by talking to the right people, starting small, and letting data drive the next step."
Short-Term (Week 1-2)
  • Define the problem in one sentence. What is it costing the business right now?
  • Talk to 5-10 stakeholders. Ask what they've tried before and why it failed.
  • Map any existing workarounds people are already using.
  • Decide what you are NOT solving first. Scope is the first trade-off.
Launch Requirements
Minimum to Ship
  • Clear problem statement
  • One sponsor or champion
  • MVP tool or process live
  • One measurable success metric
Key Factors
  • Team bandwidth constraints
  • Time pressure from leadership
  • Regulatory or compliance needs
  • Stakeholder alignment across teams
Phases
01 Foundation / MVP ⌄
  • Solve the core problem only. Ignore adjacent problems for now.
  • Use manual processes where possible. Automation comes later.
  • Pick the off-the-shelf tool that gets you moving fastest.
  • Set one SLA or success metric. Measure it immediately.
  • Get signal: is this actually solving the problem?
02 Optimize ⌄
  • Automate the most repetitive manual steps.
  • Add reporting and dashboards for visibility.
  • Establish feedback loops with adjacent teams.
  • Expand scope to adjacent problems now that core is proven.
  • Measure impact versus your Phase 1 baseline.
03 Scale ⌄
  • Full automation where ROI justifies the engineering lift.
  • Self-service capabilities so the team doesn't bottleneck.
  • Cross-team integration and shared ownership.
  • Revisit build vs buy decisions with real usage data.
  • Document the system so it outlasts any individual.
KPIs
Pre-launch: signal and willingness to adopt Time-to-value Post-launch: efficiency gain Error rate reduction

Customer Support from Scratch

No CRM. No SLAs. No process. You need to build a support function that scales and feeds product with real signal.

"I started by understanding what was actually coming in before I touched any tooling. Volume, category, channel. That shaped everything else."
Short-Term (Week 1-2)
  • Audit incoming requests: volume, categories, and which channels they come through.
  • Pick one ticketing tool and get it live. Zendesk, Intercom, Freshdesk.
  • Set one SLA to start. Example: respond within 4 hours. Not twenty SLAs. One.
  • Identify your top 3 issue categories. Everything else is lower priority.
Launch Requirements
Minimum to Ship
  • Ticketing system live
  • At least one trained agent
  • Basic issue tagging set up
  • 3-5 knowledge base articles
Key Factors
  • Volume and growth rate of requests
  • Which channels customers use
  • Team size and hiring plan
  • Integration path to product team
Phases
01 Visibility ⌄
  • Ticketing system live with basic categorization.
  • One SLA target. Measure time to first response.
  • Manual weekly reporting on top issue categories.
  • Support team briefed on workarounds for known edge cases.
02 Structure ⌄
  • Knowledge base built out to deflect common tickets.
  • SLAs differentiated by issue type and severity.
  • Bi-weekly support to product feedback loop established.
  • CSAT scoring active on resolved tickets.
03 Automate ⌄
  • AI-assisted triage and routing by category.
  • Automated alerts when ticket volume spikes unexpectedly.
  • Proactive outreach for known issues before customers contact you.
  • Full dashboard visible to product and engineering in real time.
KPIs
Time to first response Resolution time CSAT score Ticket deflection rate Repeat contact rate

Subscription Billing Platform

No billing system exists. You need to stand one up without burning engineering cycles on something that isn't your differentiator.

"Billing is not our competitive advantage. I used Stripe to get to market fast, measured what broke, and only considered building when the math justified it."
Short-Term (Week 1-2)
  • Map your actual billing requirements: currencies, cycles, proration, dunning.
  • Evaluate Stripe, Zuora, and Chargebee against those requirements.
  • Talk to finance on reporting requirements before you pick a tool.
  • Separate must-haves from nice-to-haves. Launch on must-haves only.
Launch Requirements
Minimum to Ship
  • Payment processor integrated
  • Core subscription tiers live
  • Basic dunning for failed payments
  • Finance reporting baseline in place
Key Factors
  • Transaction volume and fee math at scale
  • Engineering bandwidth: build vs buy
  • Geographic and currency requirements
  • Compliance: PCI, SOC2
Phases
01 MVP Billing ⌄
  • Stripe or equivalent integrated and taking payments.
  • Core plans live. No custom pricing or exceptions yet.
  • Basic dunning enabled. Monitor failed payment rate weekly.
  • Manual reconciliation with finance until volume justifies automation.
02 Optimize ⌄
  • Proration and upgrade/downgrade logic implemented.
  • Automated reconciliation with finance systems.
  • Cohort reporting live: MRR, churn, expansion revenue.
  • Coupon, trial, and discount management active.
03 Scale or Build ⌄
  • Revisit build vs buy when fees become a material cost.
  • Advanced dunning sequences by customer segment.
  • Revenue recognition automation for accounting compliance.
  • Multi-currency and multi-region support if needed.
KPIs
Payment success rate Failed payment recovery rate MRR Churn rate Expansion MRR

Vendor Management from Scratch

Procurement is chaos. Contracts everywhere, no tracking, no process. You need to create a vendor management function without a foundation.

"I came in with a hypothesis based on spend data, not a blank slate. I validated it with finance and legal, then built around what I confirmed."
Short-Term (Week 1-2)
  • Pull spend reports. Rank vendors by cost. That's your starting prioritization.
  • Collect all existing contracts into one place. Even a shared folder counts.
  • Interview finance and legal: what's their biggest pain right now?
  • Validate your top-10 list with finance. They may reprioritize based on operational risk.
Launch Requirements
Minimum to Ship
  • Central contract repository
  • Approval workflow (even email-based)
  • Renewal calendar for top vendors
  • Vendor scorecard template
Key Factors
  • Vendor concentration risk
  • Upcoming renewal dates
  • SLA violations already occurring
  • Cost optimization opportunities
Phases
01 Visibility ⌄
  • All contracts in one place. Top 10 vendors tracked with renewal dates.
  • Basic renewal alerts set up. No surprises.
  • Lightweight approval process for any new vendor spend.
  • Manual weekly check on upcoming renewals.
02 Process ⌄
  • Full vendor onboarding checklist standardized.
  • Quarterly vendor reviews with SLA tracking.
  • Escalation path defined when vendors miss SLAs.
  • Spend analytics dashboard visible to leadership.
03 Optimize ⌄
  • Vendor consolidation strategy implemented where overlap exists.
  • Renegotiation cycles tied to performance and spend data.
  • Preferred vendor program to streamline future procurement.
  • Automated renewal reminders with lead time built in.
KPIs
Contract coverage rate On-time renewal rate Vendor SLA compliance Cost savings from renegotiation

Support to Product Feedback Loop

The loop dies on paper when nobody owns it. What makes it real is ownership, low friction, and closing the loop so people see it matters.

"A process nobody owns is not a process. Someone's job is to pull the patterns, bring specifics to product, and tell support when it shipped."
The 5-Step Framework
  • Own it - Assign one person. If it's everyone's job it's nobody's job.
  • Surface patterns - Pull data from tickets weekly. Spot repeating issues by volume.
  • Make it specific - Don't bring vague complaints. Bring "17 tickets this week about X."
  • Close the loop - When product ships a fix, tell support. They need to see impact.
  • Measure it - Track how many support-sourced issues product actually ships.
Launch Requirements
Minimum to Ship
  • Ticketing system with tagging
  • One assigned owner
  • Bi-weekly cadence set
  • Product team buy-in confirmed
Key Factors
  • Volume of tickets to analyze
  • How fast product can absorb input
  • Quality of tagging and categorization
  • Visibility that feedback was acted on
Phases
01 Manual Loop ⌄
  • Owner manually pulls top issues weekly from tickets.
  • Top 3-5 issues brought to product in bi-weekly sync with specifics and volume.
  • Issues logged in a shared tracker visible to both teams.
  • First closed-loop notification when a fix ships from support intel.
02 Structured ⌄
  • Dashboard showing top issues by volume and trend.
  • Formal intake process: support-sourced issues added to product backlog with data.
  • Closed-loop notification system when related tickets decrease post-fix.
  • Support team sees the connection between their reporting and product decisions.
03 Systematic ⌄
  • Automated tagging surfaces patterns without manual effort.
  • Real-time visibility for product into support volume by issue type.
  • Support-sourced issues tracked through the full product lifecycle.
  • Metric tracked: percentage of support-sourced issues shipped each quarter.
KPIs
Support-to-product issue conversion rate Time from flag to shipped fix Ticket volume reduction on known issues CSAT improvement

Build vs Buy Decision

The core question: where should you spend your team's limited engineering capacity? Buy for plumbing. Build for differentiation.

"Billing is not our competitive advantage. Our product is. So I bought Stripe and freed the team to build what actually moves the needle."
Decision Framework
  • Is it core to your differentiation? If yes, lean build. If no, lean buy.
  • What's the math? Cost to buy over 1 year vs cost to build in engineering hours.
  • What's your time constraint? Buying gets you to market faster. Building takes 3-6 months minimum.
  • What's the vendor risk? If their roadmap diverges from yours, what's your exit path?
  • What's your learning stage? Buy to validate. Build once you know exactly what you need.
Trigger to Revisit and Build
  • Cost savings justify the engineering lift with clear ROI math.
  • The tool is limiting a core product capability, not just a nice-to-have.
  • Vendor reliability has become a business risk.
  • You now know exactly what you need. Pre-product-market-fit, you usually don't.
Phases
01 Buy and Validate ⌄
  • Pick the off-the-shelf option that covers your must-haves.
  • Run it for 6-12 months in production with real usage.
  • Document every limitation and gap as they surface. This is your build spec.
  • Track actual cost vs budget. Be honest about fee math at scale.
02 Optimize or Replace ⌄
  • Customize or extend the tool if it gets you 80% there cheaply.
  • Renegotiate pricing if your volume gives you leverage.
  • Evaluate alternatives if the current tool has a fundamental ceiling.
  • Reassess the build threshold with real data, not assumptions.
03 Build if Justified ⌄
  • Clear ROI case: savings exceed build cost within 12-18 months.
  • Engineering capacity available without sacrificing product roadmap.
  • Migration plan from old tool with minimal disruption.
  • Sunset timeline documented and communicated.
KPIs
Time-to-launch with bought tool Cost per unit vs build alternative Limitations encountered per quarter Engineering hours freed up

Managing Misaligned Partners

Their roadmap is not your roadmap. How do you move your priorities forward without blowing up a relationship you depend on?

"I don't go in with demands. I go in with curiosity about their constraints, and then I find the overlap."
Short-Term Actions
  • Ask directly what their priorities are. Don't assume. Understand their constraints.
  • Find the overlap: where does your need benefit their business too?
  • Frame your asks in their terms. "This reduces your support burden too."
  • Identify the minimum you actually need vs the full ask. Create room to negotiate.
  • Escalate internally if the misalignment is blocking a critical path. Don't wait.
Key Factors
Assess the Situation
  • Relationship health vs urgency of need
  • What leverage do you have?
  • What can you do without them short-term?
  • What's the cost of a damaged relationship?
What NOT to Do
  • Go in hot or accusatory
  • Escalate before trying to solve it
  • Accept misalignment indefinitely without flagging
  • Overpromise internally what the partner will deliver
Phases
01 Diagnose ⌄
  • Map their constraints honestly. Budget? Capacity? Their own customers pulling them?
  • Find quick wins that work for both sides. Prove the relationship delivers value.
  • Define the minimum you need to keep your project moving.
  • Set a clear communication cadence so surprises don't compound misalignment.
02 Align ⌄
  • Joint roadmap or shared goals document. Both sides see the same north star.
  • Regular check-ins with a defined agenda. Not just escalation calls.
  • Named escalation path if things get stuck. Agreed on upfront.
  • Celebrate shared wins. Reinforce that the relationship delivers.
03 Sustain ⌄
  • Quarterly relationship reviews. Are we still aligned? What's changed?
  • Mutual dependency built in. They need you as much as you need them.
  • Executive-level relationship maintained in parallel to working-level.
  • Contingency plan ready if the relationship breaks. You're not held hostage.
KPIs
Deliverables met vs agreed timeline Escalation frequency (should decrease) Partner satisfaction signal Blockers resolved without escalation

Shipping Something Imperfect

Perfect is the enemy of shipped. The skill is knowing what "good enough" means for your specific context, and owning that call.

"The core flow works. Customers can onboard. Support can handle edge cases manually. That's good enough. Ship it, measure it, and build on what you learn."
How to Decide "Good Enough"
  • Measure against the actual requirement, not an imaginary ideal.
  • Categorize known issues: true blockers vs annoyances. Ship with annoyances.
  • Can support handle edge cases manually for now? If yes, ship it.
  • What's the cost of NOT shipping? Lost revenue, lost learning, lost time. Factor that in.
  • Can you fix fast post-launch? If your iteration cycle is quick, the bar for "done" can be lower.
Launch Requirements
Minimum to Ship
  • Core user flow works end-to-end
  • Blockers resolved or mitigated
  • Support briefed on workarounds
  • Monitoring and alerts live
Key Factors
  • Risk of shipping vs risk of waiting
  • User trust impact if it breaks
  • Ability to iterate fast post-launch
  • Stakeholder alignment on known issues
Phases
01 Ship the MVP ⌄
  • Core flow tested and working end-to-end.
  • Known issues documented and shared with stakeholders. No surprises.
  • Support team briefed. Edge cases handled manually for now.
  • Monitoring and error alerts active from minute one.
02 Stabilize ⌄
  • Fix issues ranked by frequency and severity in real usage. Not by gut.
  • Measure actual vs expected support volume. Where did we underestimate?
  • User feedback collected and triaged into backlog.
  • Unblock the edge cases that are generating the most manual work.
03 Polish ⌄
  • Systematically address technical debt in priority order.
  • Automate edge case handling that support is still doing manually.
  • Improve performance and reliability based on real load data.
  • Revisit original scope. Is what you shipped solving the right problem?
KPIs
Error rate post-launch Support ticket volume from known issues User activation rate Time to fix critical post-launch bugs

Blockers with No Solution

Sometimes the blocker is real and the front door is locked. The skill is finding the side door without freezing or pretending.

"I don't pretend it's solvable when it's not. I name it clearly, find the adjacent path, and protect the team's momentum on everything else."
The Framework
  • Name it clearly - Tell stakeholders what you're stuck on, what you've tried, and why you're stuck. Clarity is not failure.
  • Separate what you control - Some blockers are yours to solve. Some need a decision above your level. Know which is which.
  • Find the side door - Work around it, not through it. Proxy metric, different market, manual workaround, partial solution.
  • Escalate with options - Never bring just the problem. Bring 2-3 paths forward and a recommendation.
  • Park and protect momentum - If it's genuinely stuck, document it, redirect the team to what can move, and revisit when constraints change.
Key Questions to Ask
  • Is this blocker temporary or permanent?
  • Does it block everything, or just one path forward?
  • What's the cost of waiting vs finding a workaround?
  • Who has the authority to unblock this? Have you actually asked them?
  • What assumption are we making that might not be true?
Phases
01 Diagnose the Blocker ⌄
  • Root cause analysis. Why exactly is this stuck? Get specific.
  • Who needs to be involved to move it? Name them.
  • What's the minimum needed to unblock even partial progress?
  • Try at least 2-3 alternative approaches before calling it a dead end.
02 Work Around It ⌄
  • Proxy metric if you can't get real data.
  • Different market or segment if the primary path is blocked.
  • Manual process if the automated path is stuck.
  • Partial solution to maintain momentum and demonstrate progress.
03 Resolve or Accept ⌄
  • Escalated decision made at the right level with full context provided.
  • Permanent workaround accepted and documented with rationale.
  • Team redirected to what can actually move. Don't let one blocker stall everything.
  • Revisit trigger set: "We check this again when X changes."
KPIs
Time-to-resolution or workaround % of blockers resolved vs accepted Team momentum on non-blocked tracks Escalations that required exec decision

KPIs, OKRs & Milestones

Three tools that work together. Confuse them and you measure everything but learn nothing. Use them right and they keep your team aligned, honest, and moving.

"An OKR tells you where you're going. A milestone tells you if you're on track to get there. A KPI tells you if the engine is running well day to day."
How They Fit Together
OKR
Direction
Where are we going, and how will we know when we get there? Quarterly. Qualitative objective + quantitative key results.
Milestones
Execution
Are we on track? Binary checkpoints with owners and dates. Unlock the next phase of work when hit.
KPIs
Health
How is the engine running? Ongoing measurement. Tracked weekly or monthly. Tells you when something needs attention.
How to Write OKRs
  • O (Objective) - Qualitative, inspiring, time-bound. Answers "what do we want to achieve this quarter?" Should feel slightly ambitious.
  • KR (Key Result) - 2 to 4 per objective. Quantitative and specific. Answers "how will we know we achieved it?" Must be measurable, not a task.
  • Cadence - Set quarterly. Check in monthly. 70% achievement is often considered success because KRs should be stretch targets.
  • Common mistake - Writing KRs as to-do lists. "Launch the support tool" is a milestone. "Reduce response time from 8h to 2h" is a key result.
Objective
Build a customer support function that makes customers feel heard and reduces churn from support failures
KR1Reduce average response time from 8 hours to under 2 hours by end of Q3
KR2Achieve CSAT score of 4.5 or above on 90% of resolved tickets
KR3Deflect 30% of tickets through self-serve knowledge base
Objective
Establish a vendor management function that eliminates procurement chaos and protects the business from contract risk
KR1100% of active vendor contracts collected and catalogued by Week 4
KR2Zero contract renewals missed this quarter
KR3Vendor approval process adopted by finance and legal with zero bypasses
How to Choose KPIs
  • Pick 3 to 5 max. More than that and nobody watches them.
  • Each KPI must drive a decision. If you couldn't act on it, don't track it.
  • Balance leading and lagging indicators. Leading tells you what's coming. Lagging tells you what happened.
  • Avoid vanity metrics. Total signups looks great until you see nobody activates. Track what reveals truth, not what looks good in a deck.
Leading vs Lagging
Leading
Predictive. You can influence these now. They tell you what's coming before it shows up in outcomes.
Ticket deflection rate Agent first-response time Trial activation rate Support-to-product issue conversion
Lagging
Outcome-based. Tells you what already happened. You can't change them now, but they validate your direction.
CSAT score Monthly churn rate Revenue from retained customers NPS trend
Pre-Launch vs Post-Launch KPIs
Pre-Launch
  • Signal: will people use or pay for this?
  • Activation: do they complete the core flow?
  • Early retention: do they come back in week 2?
  • Support volume: is it overwhelming or manageable?
Post-Launch
  • MRR and churn by cohort
  • Expansion revenue from upgrades
  • Error rate and uptime
  • CSAT and NPS trends over time
How to Set Milestones
  • Binary. Either done or not done. No "70% complete" on a milestone. That's a task.
  • Time-bound with a specific date. "Q3" is not a date. "July 31" is a date.
  • Owned by someone. A milestone without an owner is a wish.
  • Gates, not checkboxes. Hitting a milestone should unlock the next phase of work.
Example: Standing Up Customer Support
Wk 2
Ticketing system live and accepting requests
Owner: Head of Support
Wk 4
All support agents trained and handling tickets independently
Owner: Head of Support
Wk 6
Knowledge base live with 20+ articles covering top 10 issue categories
Owner: Ops Lead
Wk 10
CSAT tracking active and first report shared with product team
Owner: Ops Lead
Wk 12
First support-to-product feedback loop completed with issues logged in backlog
Owner: PM + Support Lead
Common Mistakes to Avoid
What Goes Wrong
  • Too many KPIs. If everything is a priority, nothing is. Pick 3 to 5 and own them.
  • KRs that are just tasks. "Launch the tool" is not a key result. "Reduce response time by 60%" is.
  • OKRs set in January and forgotten until December. Check in monthly or they're theater.
  • Milestones without owners. Someone's name goes on every milestone. No name means no accountability.
  • Tracking what's easy to measure instead of what matters. Vanity metrics make decks look good and decisions go bad.
  • Conflating milestones and KPIs. A milestone is done once. A KPI is watched forever.
Quick Reference
OKR = direction & success criteria Milestones = execution gates KPIs = ongoing health signals Leading = predictive Lagging = outcome 3-5 KPIs max
